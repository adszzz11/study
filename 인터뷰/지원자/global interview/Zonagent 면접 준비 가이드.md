## 회사 개요

**Zonagent**는 부동산 개발자들이 인허가 리스크(entitlement risk)를 명확히 이해할 수 있도록 돕는 AI 기반 플랫폼입니다.

- **미션**: Make entitlement risk instantly clear, so developers can build where America needs it most
- **핵심 기능**: 조닝 규정, 승인 이력, 정치적 신호를 하나의 플랫폼에 통합하여 데이터 기반 의사결정 지원
- **본사**: Atlanta, GA, USA
- **CTO**: Mark Jung (전 Microsoft)
- **단계**: 초기 스타트업

## 예상 질문 및 맞춤형 답변

### 1. Tell me about yourself.

> I'm a Backend and DevOps engineer with 5 years of experience in the Korean fintech industry. At Danal, one of Korea's leading payment gateway companies, I've been working on payment processing systems that handle over 100,000 transactions per minute.
> 
> As the DevOps Squad Tech Leader, I've led modernization initiatives including Kubernetes adoption and CI/CD pipeline implementation. What excites me about Zonagent is the opportunity to apply my experience in building scalable, data-intensive systems to the PropTech space—especially with an AI-powered platform that transforms complex regulatory data into actionable insights.
> 
> Beyond technical work, I'm passionate about knowledge sharing. I've been active in the Korean developer community through conference presentations and our internal writing group called 'Danal Crafters.'

---

### 2. Why do you want to join Zonagent?

> Three things drew me to Zonagent.
> 
> **First, the mission resonates with me.** Making entitlement risk clear so developers can build where it's needed most—that's solving a real, tangible problem. I've spent years making payment processes transparent and reliable; I'd love to do the same for real estate development.
> 
> **Second, the technical challenge.** Building an AI platform that integrates scattered municipal data, zoning codes, and political signals requires exactly the kind of large-scale data processing and system architecture I've been doing. Processing 100,000+ transactions per minute taught me how to build systems that are both fast and reliable.
> 
> **Third, timing.** Zonagent is at an early stage where I can make a real impact. I'm looking for an environment where I can grow beyond my current ceiling and contribute to building something meaningful from the ground up.

---

### 3. What's the most challenging technical problem you've solved?

> At Danal, we faced a critical challenge: our payment processing system needed to handle peak loads of over 100,000 transactions per minute while maintaining sub-second response times and zero data loss.
> 
> The situation was complex because we were dealing with multiple transaction types, real-time reconciliation, and strict financial compliance requirements. I led the initiative to implement a Kubernetes-based infrastructure with automated CI/CD pipelines.
> 
> **The result:** 40% improvement in deployment frequency while maintaining 99.99% uptime. More importantly, the system could now auto-scale during peak periods like holidays without manual intervention.
> 
> I believe this experience directly translates to Zonagent's needs—processing large volumes of municipal data, zoning codes, and approval histories requires similar patterns of scalable, reliable data processing.

---

### 4. How do you handle working in a fast-paced, ambiguous environment?

> Honestly, I thrive in it. At Danal, I've worked on projects where requirements changed mid-stream due to new financial regulations or sudden business pivots.
> 
> My approach is three-fold:
> 
> 1. **Build flexible architectures** that can adapt—that's why I've invested heavily in containerization and microservices.
> 2. **Communicate early and often.** When I see ambiguity, I clarify assumptions before writing code.
> 3. **Ship incrementally.** Small, frequent deployments let us learn and adjust quickly.
> 
> For a startup like Zonagent, I expect things to move fast and change often. That's actually what I'm looking for—the chance to build, learn, and iterate without the bureaucratic overhead I've experienced at larger organizations.

---

### 5. Tell me about a time you failed.

> Last year, I led a monitoring architecture project using the LGTM stack—Loki, Grafana, Tempo, Mimir. I was excited about it, spent months designing the system, and was ready to deploy.
> 
> Then organizational changes happened, and the project was cancelled. It felt like a failure because I'd invested so much.
> 
> But I learned two important lessons:
> 
> 1. **Technical excellence alone doesn't guarantee success**—you need organizational buy-in and alignment with business priorities.
> 2. **Nothing is truly wasted.** The knowledge I gained about observability and monitoring has influenced how I design systems today.
> 
> Now I make sure to validate business alignment early, not just technical feasibility.

---

> 
> **Third, global exposure.** I've spent my entire career in Korea. Working with a US-based team, solving problems for the American market—that's a new challenge I'm ready for.
> 
> Zonagent checks all three boxes.

### 8. How do you approach system scalability?

> My approach to scalability is built on three principles:
> 
> **1. Design for horizontal scaling from day one.** At Danal, I containerized our services with Kubernetes so we could add capacity by spinning up more pods, not by upgrading hardware.
> 
> **2. Identify and eliminate bottlenecks proactively.** I use monitoring and observability tools to understand system behavior under load before it becomes a problem. My experience with the LGTM stack (Loki, Grafana, Tempo, Mimir) has been valuable here.
> 
> **3. Optimize where it matters.** Not everything needs to be infinitely scalable. I focus optimization efforts on the critical path—for payments, that was transaction processing; for Zonagent, it might be the AI inference pipeline or data aggregation layer.
> 
> I'd be curious to learn more about Zonagent's current architecture and where scalability challenges are emerging.

---

### 9. Tell me about your leadership experience.

> As DevOps Squad Tech Leader at Danal, I lead a team focused on modernizing our infrastructure and deployment practices.
> 
> My leadership philosophy is **"enable, don't dictate."** I focus on:
> 
> - **Setting clear direction** while giving team members autonomy in how they achieve goals
> - **Removing blockers** so engineers can focus on engineering
> - **Building knowledge-sharing culture**—I started internal tech sessions and contribute to our writing group, Danal Crafters
> 
> One thing I've learned is that technical leadership isn't just about being the best coder. It's about multiplying the team's effectiveness. Sometimes that means writing code, sometimes it means writing documentation, and sometimes it means having difficult conversations.
